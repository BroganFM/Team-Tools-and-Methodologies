De Bono's Six Thinking Hats

Edward de Bono, 1985 · Parallel thinking for richer group decisions

Parallel Thinking
Everyone. Same hat. Same time.
🤍
White Hat
Facts & Data
What do we know? What's missing? Neutral, objective. No interpretation.
💚
Green Hat
Creativity
New ideas. Alternatives. Provocations. No judgement — generate freely.
💙
Blue Hat
Process
Facilitator hat. Sets agenda, manages the process, decides which hat is next.
💛
Yellow Hat
Optimism
Benefits, value, best-case outcomes. Why could this work? Constructive positivity.
❤️
Red Hat
Emotion & Gut
How do you feel? Instinctive reaction. No justification needed — just honest emotion.
🖤
Black Hat
Caution & Risk
What could go wrong? Due diligence. Not pessimism — responsible caution.
Facilitator tip
Always open and close with the Blue Hat. Keep each hat to 3–5 minutes. Use the Red Hat early — it legitimises emotion before the analytical hats take over.

The 5 Whys

Sakichi Toyoda · Toyota Production System · Keep asking until you hit the system, not the person

P
The Problem
The website went down during peak traffic.
1
Why?
The server ran out of memory and crashed.
2
Why?
A new background process was consuming unexpected resources.
3
Why?
It wasn't tested under production-level load before deployment.
4
Why?
The team doesn't have a staging environment that mirrors production.
5
Why? — Root Cause
A staging environment was cut from last quarter's budget without assessing the operational risk.
Do this
Stop at the system failure — the process, policy, or decision that made this outcome possible
Never stop here
"Human error" — this is almost always a symptom of a broken system, not a root cause

Point and Call (Shisa Kanko)

Japanese railway industry, late 19th century · Up to 85% reduction in human error

👁️
1. Look
Direct your eyes to the specific item, indicator, or status you are checking. Don't glance — look deliberately. Familiarity is the enemy; treat it as if you're seeing it for the first time.
☝️
2. Point
Extend your arm and point your finger at the item. The physical gesture activates a different neural pathway than vision alone — making the brain engage consciously rather than on autopilot.
🗣️
3. Call Out
Say the status aloud — clearly and specifically. "Deployment target: production. Confirmed." Speaking externalises the check, making it harder to skip and observable by others nearby.
Verbal + physical = 2× more brain activation than visual alone
✅
4. Confirm & Proceed
In a team setting, a second person responds to confirm. In a solo context, the act of calling out itself creates the necessary pause. Only then proceed. Never reverse the order.
85% error reduction in Japanese railway studies

After Action Review

US Army, 1970s · Run within 48 hours. Be honest. Write it down. Act on it.

1
What was supposed to happen?
The intent, the plan, the expected outcome. Be specific about what success looked like — and what metrics or milestones you were working toward.
2
What actually happened?
The reality — not the sanitised version. Stick to observable facts and events. This is the hardest step. Teams that skip the honesty here get nothing from the rest.
3
Why was there a difference?
The gap between intent and reality — this is where the learning lives. Use the 5 Whys here if the cause isn't immediately clear. Don't accept 'communication issues' as an answer.
4
What will we do differently?
The concrete changes that follow. Assign each to a named person with a deadline. An AAR that produces no action is just a conversation. Write it up and share it within 24 hours.
Run it
Within 48 hours of the event. With the people who did the work. 30–60 minutes maximum.
Set the tone
"This is about learning, not blame." Say it at the start. Mean it. Enforce it if you need to.

The Cynefin Framework

Dave Snowden, 1999 · Know what kind of problem you're facing before you try to solve it

Complex
Complex
Probe → Sense → Respond
No right answer exists. Cause and effect only visible in retrospect. Experiment, learn, adapt. Most leadership and culture challenges live here.
Complicated
Complicated
Sense → Analyse → Respond
Right answers exist — but require expertise to find. Call in specialists. Do the analysis. Good practice applies, not just best practice.
The Centre
Confused / Chaotic
Chaotic: Act → Sense → Respond
No discernible cause and effect. Stop analysing. Act immediately to stabilise, then move to sense-making. In the centre: figure out which domain you're actually in first.
Chaotic →
Clear
Sense → Categorise → Respond
Cause and effect are obvious to everyone. Best practice exists and should be followed. Don't over-engineer it — but don't get complacent either.
↑ Complex
Watch: Complex → Chaotic
The cliff edge
Complacency in a Complex domain can tip suddenly into Chaos. The most dangerous transition — and one most organisations miss until it's too late.
The most common mistake
Applying Complicated-domain thinking (analyse → best practice) to Complex-domain problems (culture, innovation, strategy). Analysis doesn't solve emergence — experimentation does.

RACI Matrix

Responsibility assignment · Every task needs exactly one Accountable person. Not two. One.

R — Responsible
Does the work. Can be more than one person. Accountable person delegates to them.
A — Accountable
Ultimately answerable. Signs off. There must be exactly one per task. If there are two, there are none.
C — Consulted
Input sought before decisions. Two-way communication. Limit this — too many C's creates a bottleneck.
I — Informed
Kept up to date after decisions are made. One-way communication. They don't need to be asked — just told.

Pre-mortem Analysis

Gary Klein · Imagine the project has already failed — then work backwards

1
Set the scene
"It is twelve months from today. This project has failed — completely and embarrassingly. We're in a post-mortem. Something went badly wrong." Say this clearly, then pause. Let it land.
↓
2
Write silently (2–3 mins)
Everyone writes down every reason why the project failed — individually and silently. This is the critical step. Silent writing prevents anchoring to the first idea raised and ensures quiet voices get captured.
↓
3
Collect one reason each (round-robin)
Go around the room. Each person shares one failure reason. Rotate until all reasons are captured. Duplicates are useful — frequency signals priority. Push for specificity: "scope creep" is not specific enough.
↓
4
Prioritise and assign mitigations
Rank by likelihood × impact. Take the top five failure modes and assign a named owner to each mitigation. Build these into the project plan before you leave the room. Research shows pre-mortems improve foresight accuracy by ~30%.

Nonviolent Communication

Marshall Rosenberg · Say what you need without triggering a defence response

O
Step 1
Observation
Describe what you see or hear — factually, without evaluation. Separate the data from your interpretation of it.
Not: "You're always late."
But: "You've arrived after the start time in our last three one-to-ones."
F
Step 2
Feeling
Express how you feel — using feeling words, not thoughts disguised as feelings.
Not: "I feel like you don't respect my time."
But: "I feel frustrated and undervalued."
N
Step 3
Need
Identify the underlying need that isn't being met. Needs are universal — reliability, respect, clarity, autonomy. Not about what the other person should do.
"...because I need our time together to feel reliable and respected."
R
Step 4
Request
Make a specific, actionable request — not a demand. A request leaves room for the other person to respond. Demands close conversations; requests open them.
"Would you be willing to arrive five minutes early to our next session?"
Pair with Radical Candour
RC tells you what to say — be direct, be honest, care about the person. NVC tells you how to structure it so it lands without triggering a defensive shutdown.
